## Indian mutual fund taxation (post-Budget 2024)

Taxes materially change post-tax returns, especially for investors in the 20 % or 30 % income-tax slab. Always factor this into fund selection — not just gross returns and fees.

**The 65 % equity rule.** A fund's tax treatment is determined by how much of its portfolio sits in Indian equities:

- **≥ 65 % equity → equity-taxed** (favourable):
  - Long-term (held > 1 year): 12.5 % on gains above ₹1.25 L per year
  - Short-term (held ≤ 1 year): 20 % flat
  - Applies to: Large/Mid/Small/Large & Mid/Flexi/Multi Cap, Index funds, ELSS, Aggressive Hybrid, Balanced Advantage, Dynamic Asset Allocation, Equity Savings, Arbitrage
- **< 65 % equity → slab-taxed** (all gains at investor's marginal income-tax slab, no holding-period concession, no indexation):
  - Applies to: Conservative Hybrid, Balanced Hybrid, Multi Asset Allocation, all Debt funds (Gilt, Liquid, Short Duration, Corporate Bond), Gold funds/ETFs

**ELSS is special.** Investment qualifies for Section 80C deduction up to ₹1.5 L per year (saves up to ₹46.8k tax for a 30 % slab investor), has a 3-year lock-in, and gains are equity-taxed. If the investor hasn't used their 80C limit, ELSS is usually the most tax-efficient equity wrapper for the first ₹1.5 L.

**Practical implications for plan design:**
- For a 30 % slab investor, a debt fund's 7 % pre-tax return becomes ~4.9 % post-tax. An equity fund's 12 % pre-tax return becomes ~10.5 % post-tax. The gap widens significantly.
- Prefer Aggressive Hybrid (equity-taxed) over Conservative Hybrid for hybrid exposure unless the investor specifically needs the lower volatility.
- For short-term debt needs, arbitrage funds are often more tax-efficient than liquid funds at higher slabs.
- Mention tax treatment explicitly when it changes the recommendation — don't bury it in footnotes.
